Zone A — North Beds · Spring→Summer Rotation
53 × ~14ft
Two new 4×8ft raised beds flank the diamond paver path — first thing you see coming through the main gate.

Spring (Bed 1): Carrots, beets, radish (radish marks slow carrot rows + deters cucumber beetles). Succession lettuce every 2 weeks.
Spring (Bed 2): Kale, bok choy, kohlrabi, arugula, radicchio. Sugar snap peas climb east fence.
Summer: Bed 1 → cucumbers on the paired cattle panel arch. Bed 2 → beans (direct sow — follow brassicas, great nitrogen sequence). Corn block in open ground (4×4 min). Tomatoes in existing bed with basil at feet.
🌿 Companion: Radish deters cucumber beetles — let a few bolt near the arch. Marigolds edge every bed. Nasturtiums at corners trap aphids.
💧 Soaker hose loops through both raised beds + tomato bed on timer. Corn gets drip emitters.
Zone B — Center Garden · Greens, Herbs & Flowers
53 × ~10ft
The soul of the garden — what makes it feel lush and alive from the path.

Herb cluster: Rosemary, thyme, sage, oregano grouped together (same water/sun needs, zero maintenance after year 1). Mint in a buried 5-gallon pot — it will escape otherwise.
Flower weaving: Don't row-plant flowers — scatter through every bed. Marigolds at every edge. Calendula between greens. Chamomile self-sows forever. Zinnias, stocks, celosia for height. Sunflowers anchor the south edge, facing out.
Lavender hedge: Expanded collection along west border. Fragrant hedge over time.
🌿 Chamomile improves brassica flavor nearby. Calendula attracts aphid-eating hoverflies. This zone does passive pest management for the whole garden.
💧 Herbs drought-tolerant after year 1 — no irrigation needed. Flowers get soaker hose first season only.
Zone C — South Main · Squash Empire & Corn
53 × ~12ft
Five winter squash + pumpkins — each vine runs 8–12ft. Wild and lush by August.

Paired cattle panel arch: Two panels side-by-side. Train delicata + candy roaster up and over. Hubbard, Sweet Meat, pumpkins sprawl outward.
Solo cattle panel: North fence or angled — second cucumber planting or late beans.
Three Sisters block: Corn (4×4 min) + beans at base after corn is knee-high + squash sprawling out. Self-fertilizing polyculture.
🌽 Three Sisters: corn gives structure, beans fix nitrogen (feeds corn free), squash shades soil (moisture + weed suppression). Zero fertilizer needed if done right.
💧 Drip emitters at base of each squash hill. Water deeply 2× per week — builds deep roots, reduces mid-season workload.
Zone D — West 17ft Strip · Perennial Foundation
17ft wide
Plant once, maintain almost never. This zone does its job for decades.

Peonies stay. Lavender hedge (staggered, not perfectly spaced — prune early spring, never into old wood). Native wildflower strip: echinacea, black-eyed Susan, wild bergamot, clump-forming goldenrod, native aster — blooming May through October. Knockout shrub rose as anchor. Alliums/chives at rose feet.
🌿 This strip is a permanent insectary — year-round habitat for beneficial insects that control pests throughout your vegetable beds passively.
💧 Zero irrigation after year 1. Hand water once a week to establish in 2026. That's it forever.
Zone E — Lower 20×12ft · Strawberries, Watermelon & Brassicas
20 × 12ft
South-facing slope = warmest zone — perfect for watermelon.

Strawberries (existing trellis area) — mulch with straw, consider pinching first flowers in year 1. Watermelon on the south slope with black plastic mulch. Spring brassicas (transplant 4–6 wks before last frost), then fall brassicas (start indoors mid-July, transplant August — better than spring!). Woodchip path to orchard.
🌿 Dill near brassicas attracts parasitic wasps that hunt cabbage worms. Nasturtiums trap aphids. Watermelon loves being near corn for wind shelter.
💧 Soaker hose through strawberry bed. Drip emitter per watermelon hill. Soaker through brassicas — critical during head formation.

🍅 Tomatoes + Basil
Basil at tomato feet confuses thrips and aphids with volatile oils. Also improves tomato flavor. One basil per tomato.
🌽 Three Sisters (Corn+Beans+Squash)
Corn gives structure, beans fix nitrogen free, squash shades soil suppressing weeds. The most proven polyculture in history.
🥒 Cucumbers + Radish + Nasturtium
Radish deters cucumber beetles. Let a few bolt near arches. Nasturtiums trap aphids away from cucumbers.
🥦 Brassicas + Dill + Chamomile
Dill attracts wasps that hunt cabbage worms. Chamomile improves brassica growth and flavor nearby.
🌸 Marigolds — Everywhere
French marigolds kill soil nematodes for years. Edge every bed. Repels whitefly, attracts predatory insects. Non-negotiable.
🍎 Comfrey Under Fruit Trees
Deep taproots mine potassium + calcium. Chop leaves and drop as mulch 3–4× per season. Free mineral-rich fertilizer.
🍀 White Clover Living Mulch
Fixes nitrogen into soil. Feeds apple + cherry trees free year after year. Feeds bees May through October.
💐 Calendula — Garden Healer
Attracts hoverflies whose larvae eat aphids at extraordinary rates. Self-sows forever — plant once.
💜 Lavender + Everything
Confuses cabbage moths, aphids, deer. Attracts bees from a mile away — critical for fruit tree pollination.
🧅 Alliums Near Roses
Chives/ornamental alliums at rose bases repel aphids and black spot. Bloom beautifully in spring. Dramatically reduces rose maintenance.
🌻 Sunflowers + Cucumbers
Sunflowers trap aphids away from cucumbers. Provide windbreak. Bees visiting sunflowers also pollinate cucumbers.
🫐 Elderberry Border
Windbreak for whole garden. Pollinators love flat flower clusters — critical for fruit trees. Distracts birds from other crops.
Core strategy: Single ¾" hose from house → battery timer at garden entry → ½" poly drip tubing through zones → soaker hose or emitters at plants. Timer handles beds automatically. You do a daily 10-min check with the hose for anything extra.
1. Main Hose Run
Quality ¾" rubber hose (not vinyl — cracks in cold) from house to garden. Coil slack near main gate. Connect to splitter inside garden for two zones at once.
2. Battery Timer ← Most Important Buy
Orbit or Rain Bird battery timer (~$25–40) at garden entry. Set for 5–7am watering. Beds water while you sleep. Replace batteries once a season.
3. Raised Beds — Soaker Hose
Soaker hose looped through both new raised beds + tomato bed. Even, low-pressure, no wet foliage (reduces disease). 30–45 min every other day on timer.
4. Squash, Corn, Watermelon — Drip
½" poly tubing through Zone C with 1 GPH drip emitters at each hill. Water deeply 2× per week — builds deep roots, reduces your mid-summer workload.
5. Strawberries — Soaker Hose
Soaker hose loop through strawberry bed. Mulch heavily with straw over hose — cuts evaporation 50%, keeps berries clean.
6. Your Daily Walk
Timer handles beds. Your job: 10 min daily with hose for new transplants, watermelon at fruit set, corn tasseling in dry weeks. Walk, check, enjoy.
Budget ~$200–$310 total. Buy brass fittings — not plastic — at every connection. Poly tubing and soaker hose last 5–8 years. The timer is the only thing you'll replace every few years. Fully repairable with a $3 coupler.
Canopy
Apple (A) + Cherry (C) trees — young now, canopy in 5–7 years. Mulch 3–4ft radius with wood chips immediately. Thin fruit clusters in June once bearing.
Shrubs
Elderberry ×2 (NE corner — windbreak, flowers in June, berries in August, pollinator magnet). Currants ×2 (mid-border, shade-tolerant, prolific). Blueberries ×2 varieties (amended acid bed, pH 4.5–5.5, fruit more heavily every year for 20+ years).
Herbs
Comfrey Bocking 14 — 1 crown per fruit tree, 2ft from trunk. Chop leaves 3–4× per season, drop as mulch. Sterile — won't spread. Lavender along path edge — critical for pollinator attraction during apple/cherry blossom.
Ground
White clover broadcast throughout — fixes nitrogen, feeds trees free, feeds bees continuously. Strawberries as groundcover between trees. Creeping thyme along path edges — walkable, fragrant, drought-proof.
Roots
Daffodil + allium bulbs — plant in fall 2026 throughout orchard. Emerge in spring, deter deer + rodents (both toxic to them), flowers are stunning. Foliage disappears by summer. Once-and-done planting that grows bigger every year.
Year 1: Sparse — normal. Year 2: Strawberries fruit, currants produce first crop, elderberry flowers. Year 3–4: Blueberries producing seriously, elderberry full harvest, clover permanently established. Year 5+: A real food forest — self-managing, harvesting May through October.
Golden rule: All roses below are disease-resistant — no spraying required. Plant alliums/chives at every rose's feet. Mulch well. That's the maintenance.

Climbing Roses

Climbing · Zone 5–9 · Repeat Bloomer
🌹 'New Dawn'
Most famous climbing rose. Soft blush-pink, incredible fragrance, blooms June then repeats in fall. Covers your east fence in 3 years. Nearly indestructible. Edible hips in fall.
Climbing · Zone 5–9 · Thornless!
🌹 'Zéphirine Drouhin'
The only truly thornless climber. Deep cerise-pink, intensely fragrant. Perfect for the cattle panel arch — no thorns means no snagging as you walk through daily. Repeat bloomer.

Shrub Roses

Shrub · Zone 4–9 · Bulletproof
🌸 'Carefree Beauty'
Large warm pink blooms, very fragrant, blooms all season without deadheading, zero disease. 4–5ft rounded shrub. Large orange hips in fall that birds love.
Shrub · Zone 4–9 · Native Species
🌸 Rosa carolina (Pasture Rose)
Native rose of the eastern US. Non-invasive, single pink blooms loved by native bees, brilliant red hips. 3ft, spreads slowly by suckers (easily managed). Wild and natural feel.
Shrub · Zone 4–9 · Continuous Bloom
🌸 'Knock Out' Rose
Most disease-resistant rose ever developed. Blooms May through frost without deadheading. Cherry red or pink. Virtually impossible to kill. 6 months of color, zero effort.
Shrub · Zone 4–8 · Fragrant + Edible Hips
🌸 'Thérèse Bugnet'
Rugosa hybrid — spicy fragrant, lilac-pink, repeats all season. Large edible hips high in Vitamin C. Thrives in poor soil. Gorgeous fall color. Almost no care needed.
East fence: 'New Dawn' — train horizontally for maximum bloom coverage.
Cattle panel arch: 'Zéphirine Drouhin' — one on each side, thornless for daily walk-through.
Zone D west border: 'Carefree Beauty' + 'Knock Out' as anchors. Rosa carolina at the wild end.
Food forest corner: 'Thérèse Bugnet' rugosa — edible hips, feeds birds, looks stunning.
Water drains straight out — so this needs plants that genuinely thrive in dry, well-drained, full-sun conditions. In zone 6b this gives you a beautiful drought-tolerant palette.
💜 Lavender
1–2 plants as central anchor. Spills beautifully over tub edge. Fragrant, drought-proof, pollinators adore it.
🌿 Creeping Thyme
Spreads to fill gaps. Fragrant when brushed. Tiny pink flowers. Walkable and virtually indestructible.
🌸 Sedum 'Autumn Joy'
Perennial, structural, turns copper-red in fall. Pollinators love it. Zero care — cut back in early spring.
🌸 Echinacea
One plant as a tall statement. Native, zero care, pollinators love it. Seedheads feed birds in winter.
🌼 Yarrow (native)
Ferny foliage, flat flower heads, butterflies adore it. Yellow or white. Extremely drought-tolerant once established.
🌺 Portulaca (annual filler)
Spills over edges in jewel colors. Thrives in heat and drought. Self-sows modestly. No deadheading needed.
Zero irrigation needed. No deadheading required for most of these. Just cut back in early spring once a year. This becomes a permanent pollinator magnet right next to the coop.

Get Your Garden App Online

The easiest free way to get a real link you can bookmark on your phone and share with anyone — no coding required. Takes about 10 minutes.

Best option for you: GitHub Pages. It's free forever, gives you a real URL like yourusername.github.io/my-garden, works on any phone or computer, and your notes + task checkoffs save locally in your browser on each device.

Step-by-Step: Get Your Link in 10 Minutes

1
Download this file. Click the download button above to save garden-app-2026.html to your computer.
2
Create a free GitHub account. Go to github.com and sign up. It's free and takes 2 minutes. Choose any username — it'll appear in your URL.
3
Create a new repository. Click the + in the top right → "New repository". Name it my-garden. Set it to Public. Check "Add a README file". Click "Create repository".
4
Upload your garden file. In your new repository, click "Add file" → "Upload files". Drag your garden-app-2026.html file in. Important: Rename it to index.html before uploading (or rename it on your computer first). Click "Commit changes".
5
Turn on GitHub Pages. In your repository, click "Settings" → "Pages" (in the left sidebar). Under "Source", select Deploy from a branch. Set branch to main and folder to / (root). Click Save.
6
Wait 2–3 minutes, then visit your link. Your garden app will be live at: https://yourusername.github.io/my-garden. Bookmark it on your phone's home screen for instant access.
7
Add to your phone's home screen. On iPhone: open the link in Safari → tap the Share button → "Add to Home Screen". On Android: open in Chrome → tap the 3-dot menu → "Add to Home Screen". Now it works like an app!
Important about notes + tasks: Your notes and task checkoffs save to your browser's local storage on each device. They'll persist as long as you use the same browser and don't clear your browser data. For now this means notes on your phone are separate from notes on your computer — that's a limitation of this simple approach. If you want notes synced across devices in the future, ask Claude about adding a simple backend (it's a next step, not necessary now).

To Update Your App in the Future

If you want to make changes — add crops, update notes in the plan, etc. — just download the updated file from Claude, rename it index.html, and re-upload it to your GitHub repository. GitHub Pages automatically updates your live link within a few minutes.
